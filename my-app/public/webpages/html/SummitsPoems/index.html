Sapling

A single sapling stood alone
In the middle of the woods,
Estranged from all the others
And the closeness they had grown.

Visitors would come and look
And shake their heads in pity,
Admiring the pairs of oaks
That stood beside the brook.

“Such a shame, that lonely tree,”
They whispered to each other.
“Perhaps someday a wind blown seed
Will bring a mate to thee.”

The seasons passed and sun and rain
Nourished the little tree,
Encouraging the growth of leaf
And twig and bud and vein.

Through the years it spread and grew,
Though painfully alone,
And spread its boughs o’er those around,
Withstood the winds that blew.

One day those visitors returned,
And couldn’t help but marvel
At the lonely sapling they had spurned:
A lovely, flaming maple.

Solitude

The crisp fall breeze disturbs the leaves,
And sends them whirling through the trees;
Reds and oranges, browns and greens,
Aspen, willow, oak and beech.

Most along the wayside scattered,
Others floating worn and tattered,
Over hills and places flatter,
O’er the gentle brook that chatters.

Then at last the brisk wind dies,
Giving up its mournful cry.
A single leaf descends from flight,
And comes to rest within my sight.

The golden scrap evokes a smile
As I watch it. All the while,
The chill creeps underneath my layers,
Urging me to join the players.

But just one moment ere I go,
And leave the lonely wind to blow,
Before the cares of life intrude,
I’ll enjoy this solitude.

Upon Returning

I walk inside and drop my bags,
Body overcome by jet lag.
This familiar place feels strange now
And subtle changes remind me how
Long I’ve been away.

I hug my family awkwardly.
They too have changed, it seems to me.
Simultaneously, though,
Their mannerisms are so
Familiar to me.
Have they changed or is it I
Who have been altered by this time?
The differences it wrought in me
Are not so obvious to see;
The work of subtlety.

A cross road stands before me now
And not the kind observed by cows.
Will I become the girl I was
Or embrace the changes made because
I’m better for their touch?

Reflections on Thanksgiving

A day of feasting, friends, and noise
Spent indulging in savory joys;
An afternoon nap on the couch while the kids
Scatter the carpet with all of their toys.

Such is the general picture we see
Of what Thanksgiving ought to be.
But I think there is much more meaning
Than simply partaking in gluttony.

I’d venture to say that it’s all in the name
And the giving of thanks is the key to this game;
But it might be prudent to inquire
What hold on Christians this holiday claims.

The Mayflower pilgrims began this whole thing
On a journey which religious freedom did bring.
They sailed across the ocean blue
And barely survived through winter to spring.

After a bountiful harvest that year,
With some of the natives they gathered near
To thank their maker for his abundant provision
With hearts full of gratitude, wonder, and cheer.

Then into the misty past it faded
Though by Congress some words on it were traded
Until Abraham Lincoln declared it official
In the midst of a war full of violence and hatred.

But regardless of my nationality,
I think this day has much claim on me
Because of the outpouring of grace
Bestowed freely and fully upon me.

The sentiments are tied to Christmas and Easter,
I’m grateful for the baby asleep in the feeder;
The Son of God who lived sinless on earth
To save me from hell when I could do neither.

His mutilated body displayed,
The holes in his hands as he spread them and bade
Me run to his arms despite
The mistakes and choices I’ve made.

So behind the faces and foods, I see
The blood-soaked grain of the mercy tree
Where Jesus Christ suffered torture
For me.

Last Day

I came to Echo and age ago,
Or so it seems to me.
And since that time, my family’s grown
From eight to thirty-three.

The faces that were once so strange,
It pains me now to leave;
So unconscious was this change
That I can scarce believe
A few scant weeks—three months!—ago,
These dear friends I’d hardly known.

The lodges look empty and sad today,
Almost unwelcoming
Devoid of friendliness and play
As if they, too, are mourning.

I linger in the open air
With others as we say,
“Goodbye!” “Drive safely!” “Take care!”
I wish a different day
Would carry all of us away.
I dearly want to stay.

But now, at last, my turn has come
To leave this blessed place.
Though the atmosphere is somewhat glum,
I revel in this grace:
I’ll see these faces once again
When we come to Summit’s den.

I relish embraces and blessings received,
Step into the vehicle
And turn in time to see
My receding family waving to me.

Musical Dilemma

Music is a form of art,
Capable in whole or part,
Of expressing such views as
The piece’s author has.

Music is a powerful thing,
Driving deep the words we sing,
Even when unconsciously
Seared into the memory.

Sometimes music creeps inside,
And unnoticed in the lyrics hide
Lies the devil tells us all,
Buried in the music’s call.

Scripture says to fix my eyes
Where purity and honor lie,
To think on truth and excellence,
The things of God, in essence.

Songs of sex and drunkenness
Fail this test, though I confess,
I have found enjoyment in
Taylor Swift and Morgan Wallen.

How is one supposed to be
In this world but not its seed?
How is the work of Christ in me
On display for them to see?

Ought I to take pleasure in
Songs that glorify our sin?
When these words aloud I sing,
Do I honor the one true King?

When my inner soundtrack with them rings
Am I thinking on the things
That are good and lovely?
Is this hypocrisy?

Can immoral lyrics be redeemed?
Can a song that seemed
Beautiful in form yet filthy in speech
Have a worthy lesson to teach?

And if such a message it had to show,
Would that fact alone condone
Allowing its residence
Into my mind and home?
